# Prototype Evaluation Report

This prototype generates sales follow-up emails from a small structured case record. The intended user is a sales representative or account executive who wants a fast first draft after a demo, discovery call, trade show conversation, or stalled opportunity. The input is a compact set of CRM-style fields: prospect name, company, product, last interaction, and freeform notes, plus an internal evaluation rubric in the test set. The output is a ready-to-edit follow-up email. The value is straightforward: it can save reps time, improve consistency, and make it easier to send timely follow-ups after customer interactions. In the best cases from this evaluation, the prototype produced usable drafts that were well structured, polite, and clearly tied to the prospect’s stated concerns.

The model choice is `gpt-4o`. That is a reasonable fit for this prototype because the task is short-form business writing with light synthesis rather than deep domain reasoning. `gpt-4o` is strong at producing polished, concise text and usually follows formatting and tone instructions well, which matters for customer-facing email. It is also a pragmatic choice for a product prototype because it is widely used, fast enough for interactive drafting, and capable of handling incomplete notes better than smaller or older models. In this project, the main issue was not fluency; the issue was factual discipline.

I ran the app end to end on April 6, 2026 against all five cases in `eval_set.json` using the final prompt in `app.py`, then ran the same five cases again with the baseline prompt from Version 1 in `prompts.md`. The final prompt was clearly better than the baseline on structure and, in several cases, on grounding. Version 1 often wrote polished but more speculative sales language. The final prompt usually produced shorter, cleaner emails and more often stayed close to the provided notes. A concrete example is `sales_followup_normal_01`. The baseline output said, “For a team of 25, we have tailored solutions to ensure a smooth transition,” which is not supported by the input. The final prompt removed that claim and instead said, “I’m here to provide any details you need” about implementation timeline and pricing. That is a meaningful improvement because it answers the prospect’s stated interest without inventing packaging or onboarding commitments.

Another useful comparison is the sparse-information case, `sales_followup_hard_sparse_03`. The baseline version said, “I enjoyed the brief conversation we had,” which implies a more substantial exchange than the notes support. The final version was still generic, but it was safer: it mainly referenced meeting at the trade show and invited Avery to continue the conversation. This shows the revised prompt is directionally better at avoiding hallucinated context when information is thin. The tradeoff is that the safer output can become bland, but for this use case that is preferable to making up facts.

That said, the final prompt does not reliably solve the core risk. In `sales_followup_multi_stakeholder_04`, the generated email claimed that “our clients typically see a significant reduction in missed sales opportunities and an increase in operational efficiency,” even though no customer proof points or performance claims were provided. It also described the integration process as streamlined and minimally disruptive without evidence in the case input. In `sales_followup_competitor_05`, the email said ClearPipeline has an intuitive interface and rapid implementation, which may be plausible, but those product claims were not in the input. In `sales_followup_hard_sparse_03`, the email referred to Avery’s “objectives,” even though the notes explicitly say no pain points or requirements were captured. Across the five final outputs, the model was consistently fluent, but it still overstated product value or inserted lightly fabricated claims in multiple cases. Based on this run, I would describe the prototype as useful but not yet trustworthy enough for autonomous external communication.

The prototype still needs human review because the remaining failure mode is subtle, not catastrophic. The model usually does not invent absurd facts; instead, it adds believable sales phrasing that sounds normal in context but may still be unapproved or unsupported. That is exactly the kind of error a busy seller might miss when skimming a draft. It also tends to default to generic value claims instead of explicitly acknowledging missing information, even though the prompt instructs it to surface gaps. A human should review for unsupported claims, unwanted promises, and tone fit before anything is sent. I would also want a stronger evaluation set before deployment, especially cases with compliance-sensitive language, pricing ambiguity, missing decision-maker information, and products where feature claims must be tightly controlled.

My recommendation is to deploy this only as an internal drafting assistant, not as an auto-send tool. It is good enough to help a salesperson get to a first draft faster, but not good enough to represent the company without review. If deployed, it should be gated by three conditions: every email must be human-approved before sending, the input form should be more structured so the model has less room to infer missing facts, and the outputs should be checked against an explicit “no unsupported claims” rubric during QA. I would not recommend fully automated outbound use in its current form. The evidence from this evaluation suggests the prototype has product value, but only in a human-in-the-loop workflow where speed matters more than perfect reliability and where a seller is accountable for the final message.
